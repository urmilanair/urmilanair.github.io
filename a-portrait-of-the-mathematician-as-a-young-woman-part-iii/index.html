A portrait of the mathematician as a young woman, Part III

June 30, 2021


Description

This is my translation of an article written by the mathematician Michèle Vergne. In it, Vergne reflects on her youth and how she became a mathematician in a sexist world. There are no stories of violence or male brutality to be had here. Vergne writes instead of the winsome face of patriarchy—a face that is, in fact, only too familiar to anyone who has lived in a state of society. This is the third and final part of my translation of the article.

Born in 1943, Vergne studied and worked in France and the United States, becoming a professor at MIT, at the École normale supérieure de jeunes filles, and a director of research at CNRS (the French National Centre for Scientific Research). Her field of mathematical research is representation theory.

The article was originally presented in 1975 at a seminar on “Mathematics, Mathematicians and Society” in the Math department of what is today the University of Paris-Saclay at Orsay.

My thanks to Michèle Vergne for permission to publish this translation on my blog. The original article in French may be found on her website:
https://webusers.imj-prg.fr/~michele.vergne/


Note: Part I and Part II of my translation of Vergne’s article, were previously published on this blog.


The article translated, part III


“What am I doing here?”

Once I began to make progress in mathematics, once I knew that I could complete my thesis, bizarrely enough, I entered a period of extreme depression. I shall not analyze at all why was I so depressed. It was roughly a feeling of “what I am doing here?” I felt as if I had had great difficulty in wanting to arrive somewhere, only to find it empty. What I had searched for was an apology for being female. It was a search for proof of my existence through the affective and intellectual recognition of the only sources of recognition: men. And well, I did not find that recognition there—at least, not as I desired it. I had work relations that were completely alienating. But for someone to recognize me, to love me and for me to find myself, thanks to my theorems, placed in a certain fixed place within a one-dimensional universe … And now, pooh! It still is rather empty … the dissociation between my “personality” and my work … I continue to work in a very alienated manner—to please not myself, but those who bring recognition: men. But perhaps I no longer depend totally on their appreciation as I did before. I have a measure of esteem for myself.

My problems as a woman cannot disappear because, in effect, they exist: I think I continue to be isolated, being a woman. There is discrimination—overt or insidious, voluntary or involuntary—against all women. In informal communications, women are often excluded. Take the case of the Bourbaki congress … Even when women “have succeeded,” they are never allowed to acquire a feeling of belonging to the profession, that feeling of professional solidarity. I do not say that such a feeling is good or bad, but simply that it does not play a role in the same fashion in case of women.

At a meeting, in a conference, mathematicians feel obliged to interrupt their mathematical conversation if you arrive in their group, and to engage in pleasantries. In general, it is very tedious and varyingly heavy if you are a single, “free” woman, and in case of a married woman, a sudden and considerable interest is expressed—in your children rather than in your theorems. You are never approached for a discussion of an interesting mathematical result, because interesting results are only brought up with people who are themselves interesting and susceptible of offering something in exchange, and therefore certainly not with a woman—one may approach her to discuss life’s difficulties, or its numerous joys … I spent a year at Berkeley—receiving nothing but compliments on my abilities as a mathematician, coming from people who, as I well knew, knew strictly nothing about my work. Nevertheless, they acted as if they judged it extremely interesting, though never interesting themselves to the point of wishing to discuss it, whether in private or public conversations.

Well-known, white, male mathematicians

I still feel defensive about my value as a mathematician, and once again, I think I am right to feel thus. At the least faux pas, the guillotine’s blade will fall: “They told me she was good, but really, she isn’t as good as all that.” One has no credibility. One’s words to one’s students are never ringed with its halo, as is the case with the words of well-known, white, male mathematicians. If a student chooses you as a thesis adviser, it means that that student is slightly self-deprecating. As I think I have made clear, it isn’t necessarily the most uninteresting of people who are self-deprecating … Nevertheless, thesis advising becomes more difficult. I once said to a mathematician that his demonstration was false, and I explained why. He returned to me two days later saying, “Yes, my demonstration was perhaps false, because Dixmier told me that Michel Duflo had told him it was false.”

It is very difficult to create valuable mathematical relations with other mathematicians outside a very tiny circle that knows you well. First of all, the other lacks the motivation. Then, there are always the stereotypes of paternalism, so difficult to get away from, involving as they do the underlying notion of female inferiority. Effectively, if one says something stupid, one needs a crazy amount of time to make a comeback. And one can never be brilliant, that being the rule of the game imposed on women who wish to distance themselves from paternalistic stereotypes. I have worked these past several years with a well-known mathematician named Hugo Rossi—a wonderful person and in our exchanges, after a certain length of time, we had completely distanced ourselves from the stereotypes in relation to the work we were doing. We had a very equal relationship in practice. However, 90% of mathematicians who see our articles will—without having read them, of course—have the following opinion: he was the one who did all the work. Of course, they will not say this to me, and will even invite me to the conferences to expound on our work, because it is always surprising to see a woman speak of mathematics, always amusing, like watching a dog walk on its hind limbs. When I open my mouth to speak on these occasions, I feel a certain anguish in them—and I too feel anguished, though not for the same reasons. They are nervous, then surprised, and finally relieved to see that I had nevertheless been intelligent enough to understand what they had done.

In any case, I think that if I write an article with some guy, whether he be famous or not, it is he who, outside a very small circle, will receive the main credit for the article, the real experience and what one says of it being of little consequence. Imagine that my collaborator extraordinarily declares, “She was the one who did it all.” Nobody will believe him. They will say he is too modest. If I were to say, “He was the one who did it all,” it would certainly not be modesty but rather a description of sad reality.

How many times has one heard it said of a taciturn, shy, male mathematician, “Oh, he is really very agreeable, he is good and so modest.” By contrast, I have never heard of modest female mathematician. If a female mathematician says nothing, it is because she really has nothing to say. One cannot assume for a second that she would have the nerve to hide what she thinks.

Now I, most of the time, during a seminar or a mathematical conversation, I do not say what I think, because I am afraid. And, in fact, I have interiorized completely this sexist stereotype that such and such certainly has ideas that are far more interesting than my own, and that it would be far better for me to listen. And thus in a mathematical conversation, I am not interesting, I am defensive, I never put myself forward. I speak, perhaps, of things that I know, but I would be too fearful to risk imagining myself as better than someone. And in fact, my very fruitful collaboration with Hugo Rossi was through letters: faced with a letter, I could think, but face to face with someone, I am blocked.

In conclusion—what? One must learn to have a measured esteem for oneself. Reject sterile comparisons. Reject the interiorization of what the other—the male—says, of what he does, the idea that his destiny is inherently more interesting. Reject one’s own sacrifice to that destiny, whether material or mental. Avoid contempt for oneself and for the category to which one belongs. This is difficult when one belongs to a category that is generally despised, that of black mathematicians, Algerian mathematicians, women mathematicians, provincial mathematicians, from Brittany or even from the impoverished suburbs. To put it briefly, these individuals would be the majority, if many researchers rejected the interiorization of elitist stereotypes.
