Description

This is my translation of an interview given by French philosopher and sociologist Edgar Morin to the newspaper Le Monde in November 2020. In the interview, Morin analyzes the latest ideological fractures that rend France. This is the first part of my translation of the interview.

Biographical note on Edgar Morin

Born in 1921, and renowned for his contributions to the fields of both sociology and philosophy, Edgar Morin is the holder of thirty-eight honorary doctorates from universities across the world. The emeritus research director of the French National Center for Scientific Research (CNRS) is a prolific author whose writings include the treatise Method: Towards a Study of Humankind, translated by J. L. Roland Bélanger (Peter Lang, 1992, 435 pp.). The treatise is the first of six volumes published in French under the title La Méthode (Seuil, 1977-2004). His most recent work, written in collaboration with Sabah Abouessalam, is Changeons de voie. Les leçons du coronavirus (Denoël, 160 pp.). The title may be translated as A Change of Path: Lessons from the Coronavirus. The book offers a contemplation of “the world after” (le monde après).


The Interview

It has been five years since the 2015 Charlie Hebdo and Bataclan attacks. Yet the killings continue in France, in the name of a god. The recent murder of high school teacher Samuel Paty, the stabbings in Nice, are these signs of history repeating itself?

Before I consider these tragic events, I think it is important that I situate my perspective and render explicit ‘from where’ I speak in this interview–as was enjoined on one in the past.

As far as religion is concerned, I believe human minds create gods to worship and obey. I am what one would call an agnostic. Or rather, I think the mysteries of the universe elude our minds. I consider the Bible–foundational text that it is of, Judaism, Christianity and Islam–to be woven of legends and myths. The Gospels and the Quran are equally, for me, half-legend, half-historic. I admire Jesus Christ without believing in his resurrection.

I abhor the hatred aroused towards the impious, the non-believers, the adherents of other faiths, when one religion is all-powerful, as is the case today in Iran and Saudi Arabia. I abhor the restrictions it imposes, particularly on women. This was true of Judaism in the past, and it remains so among orthodox Jews. This was true of Christianity for centuries. It remains true of numerous Muslim countries.

Yet, I do not confuse Islam with jihadism: the pious Muslim and the murderous fanatic are worlds apart, as were St. Francis of Assisi and Torquemada. The word Islamism obscures their myriad differences with its exclusive focus on proselytism and the rejection of democracy and secularism. Certainly, Sharia law is incompatible with the laws of a secular Republic. However, the majority of French Muslims accept the laws of the Republic, and the believers among them are all the more peaceable in that they view their religion as one of peace.

Islam appears to the French to be a foreign religion. This it is, by virtue of its origins and its language, Arabic. Nevertheless, Islam is a Judeo-Christian religion: it is rooted in the Bible and considers Jesus as one of its prophets.

I have a horror of all murderous fanaticisms, such as those that plagued humanity in the 20th century, and that take root anew in the guise of traditional religious forms. I enjoy involved dialogues with believers but would hate to offend them. My credo is: never offend or humiliate. Respect for others requires that one never ridicule that which is sacred to them. I do, nevertheless, arrogate to myself the right to critique their convictions: respect for freedom includes respect for my freedom of speech.

On the one hand, I feel the pain of Native Americans at the desecration of their sacred sites by European colonizers. On the other hand, when a religion is all-powerful and condemns as blasphemy any and all disobedience, I side with the condemned. I am thinking here, for instance, of the Chevalier de la Barre, the eighteenth-century French nobleman who was tortured and beheaded for having refused to salute a religious procession, or of the Ayatollah’s fatwa against Salman Rushdie.

Whence the seeming paradox: I support the freedom of women to unveil in Iran as well as the freedom of women to veil in France. This, then, is ‘from where’ I speak: I am neither Islamist nor leftist, but rather a follower of Montaigne and Spinoza. Thus, I wish for us to examine the present situation in all its complexity. Note that this does not in any way attenuate the condemnation of the murderous fanaticism of Islamic jihadists.

What do you think of the republication of the caricatures of the Prophet Mohammed, and of their use for pedagogic, political and ideological ends?

Let us begin by recalling that the caricatures of Mohammed were not a French, but rather a Danish creation. These caricatures posit an umbilical link between the Prophet, founder of Islam, revered by pious Muslims, and the jihadist terrorists of today. Such a link is, at the very least, debatable. These caricatures have not been reproduced in liberal democracies such as the UK and the US, nor in Italy and Spain where the law prohibits insults to religion.

Now the publication of caricatures of Mohammed is legal in France, even if practicing Muslims consider it blasphemous: we have the right and freedom to blaspheme. Charlie Hebdo only continues, and perhaps amplifies, a long-standing French tradition of anticlericalism and religious libertarianism, one that has flourished since the times when the Church was powerful in our society. This tradition of anti-Christianism has faded with the Church’s acceptance of secularism and is today obsolete.

The satirical weekly reproduced the caricatures in 2006, provoking a mix of laudatory and critical reactions. Among the latter was a complaint lodged by Muslim associations, which was rejected after a trial in 2007. In 2011, the Charlie Hebdo premises suffered an arson attack which was later forgotten.

The 2015 attacks engendered a shift in the meaning of both the weekly itself, and of the caricatures: Charlie Hebdo was no longer a satirical magazine but rather a symbol of freedom of expression. The journalists assassinated became, and rightly so, martyrs to liberty. And the Danish caricatures were incorporated into the French national heritage.

Then, during the trial of the perpetrators of the Charlie Hebdo attacks, the magazine decided to republish the caricatures. This republication became even an epitome of French freedom, and their diffusion became a salutary defense of critical thinking.

One teacher, high on freedom, thought of using the caricatures to cultivate critical thinking among his students. Initially, this did not incite much of a reaction, apart from a complaint by one Muslim father, and an appeasement, apparently successful, by the school’s headmistress. However, a mosque preacher stoked the embers, rekindling them to the point of provoking a young Chechen to perpetrate the gruesome jihadist action of beheading.

This murder provoked a strong emotional reaction among teachers, and across French society. It unleashed denunciations of the government, as being soft on such crimes, and of Islamo-leftist indulgence. Note, incidentally, that the latter is an imaginary construct uniting two terms, both of which are considered horrific. President Emmanuel Macron reaffirmed the fundamental Republican value of freedom and ended his speech–as per the version circulated by the media–with the promise that France would defend the caricatures, as if that were some sort of national duty. He subsequently denied and softened his stance in an interview with the Arab television channel Al-Jazeera, where he affirmed that he understood how the caricatures might shock some people.

After the initial shock and the unanimous condemnation of the attacks, critiques began to emerge of the transformation of the Danish caricatures into a national emblem of French identity. Are these critiques valid?

The horror of the beheading of the teacher Mr. Paty, after that of the Charlie Hebdo attacks–by virtue of its palpability, its cruelty, and its sheer madness–has obscured an important part of the reality from which it emerged. The sheer horror of the crime disallows any attempt at reflection and contextualization. It is as if such attempts themselves involved the sin of justifying the crime. Yet, we must not forget that such caricatures shock pious Muslims. Worse yet, these caricatures have provoked acts of murderous insanity.

Furthermore, the sanctioning of the caricatures has provoked innumerable, frenzied anti-French protests across the Islamic world. While there are certainly instances where one must, perforce, brave the incomprehension of others, there are equally instances where one does better not to provoke or excite that incomprehension, especially in times of extreme international tension.

One has to be careful of the possible perverse effects of well-intentioned actions. There is sometimes a contradiction between freedom and responsibility of speech or writing; this is one such instance, and we must know that our choice involves a risk. There is sometimes a coincidence between responsibility and irresponsibility. Thus, it seems to me irresponsible to assume the responsibility of supposing that the propagation ad infinitum of the Danish caricatures represents the heart of French freedom.

In my view, and this is an idea that I develop in the fifth volume of my treatise La Méthode (The Method), ethics cannot be restricted to good intentions. Instead, ethics must involve a sense of the consequences of one’s actions, these consequences being often contrary to the intentions underlying one’s actions. Above all, any decision taken in an uncertain, conflictual context involves the risk of engendering contrary effects. Thus, we cannot judge the caricatures exclusively by the liberating or religious libertarian intentions of their authors and broadcasters. These caricatures must equally be judged based on the possibilities of their harmful or disastrous consequences.

Freedom of expression must not exclude the prevision of misunderstandings, incomprehension, violent or criminal consequences that it can provoke. Can these caricatures cause believers to doubt their beliefs? Absolutely not. Can they contribute to the weakening of jihadism? Absolutely not.
