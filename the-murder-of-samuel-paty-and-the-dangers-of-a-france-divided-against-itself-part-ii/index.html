Description

This is my translation of an interview given by French philosopher and sociologist Edgar Morin to the newspaper Le Monde in November 2020. In the interview, Morin analyzes the latest ideological fractures that rend France. This is the second part of my translation of the interview.

Biographical note on Edgar Morin

Born in 1921, and renowned for his contributions to the fields of both sociology and philosophy, Edgar Morin is the holder of thirty-eight honorary doctorates from universities across the world. The emeritus research director of the French National Center for Scientific Research (CNRS) is a prolific author whose writings include the treatise Method: Towards a Study of Humankind, translated by J. L. Roland Bélanger (Peter Lang, 1992, 435 pp.). The treatise is the first of six volumes published in French under the title La Méthode (Seuil, 1977-2004). His most recent work, written in collaboration with Sabah Abouessalam, is Changeons de voie. Les leçons du coronavirus (Denoël, 160 pp.). The title may be translated as A Change of Path: Lessons from the Coronavirus. The book offers a contemplation of “the world after” (le monde après).


The Interview, Part II

We have heard writers and polemicists as well as ministers support the view that “Islamo-leftism” helps arm terrorism intellectually. Is this charge justified? Why is this ideological offensive occurring?

What is truly terrible is that this affair has amplified the progress of Manichaean, unilateral, reductive thinking. Any resistance towards a growing Islamophobia comes to be viewed as a sign of abject Islamo-leftism or even of complicity with the murderers. Of course, Islamo-leftism is neither Islamic nor leftist. And, unfortunately, as in 1914, in 1933, in 1940, as at every moment of collective frenzy, there are philosophers at the forefront of the hysteria.

Most dangerous of all is the fact that, as has happened several times in the past, France is divided against itself. In the best of cases, as at the start of the 20th century, a Republican and secular France vanquished a monarchical, Catholic and conservative France. In the worst of cases, as in 1940, a reactionary France prevailed, fostering a military disaster.

The lockdown has imposed a provisional freeze on the conflict which would otherwise have exploded in France. What will happen after the lockdown ends? What new political decompositions and recompositions might we anticipate? An identitarian France already stands in opposition to a humanist France. All this merits not imprecation, but rather examination and reflection.

Does the United States, divided as it stands today, foreshadow the conflict between these two opposed Frances during the next presidential elections?

As of right now, we do not know if, after Joe Biden’s victory, Donald Trump is going to attempt to stage a coup to hold on to the presidency. Tensions are high in the U.S. and I do not know if we will see an explosion or a slow pacification.

On this side of the Atlantic, for the moment, the two Frances have not yet crystallized. There will be further political decompositions and recompositions. I can well imagine the possibility of a political movement built around public welfare, which would unite the well-intentioned across the board, in search of a new economic, social and ecological way. However, I do not see it embodied at present, not in any organization, nor in any political leader. On the left, I see muddled attempts at regroupment. By contrast, I see the possibility of the emergence of an outsider representing order and discipline, that is to say, the other France, someone like General Pierre de Villiers for example. However, nothing is certain, and we definitely have many a surprise in store for us next year.

How do we avoid such dislocation?

As an adolescent, I was a member of a small party called the Mouvement des étudiants frontistes (Frontist Students Movement) that fought on two fronts simultaneously, against fascism and against Stalinism. Under the Occupation, I became a Communist. Six years later, I gave up my Communist leanings and found myself once again engaged in a fight on two fronts, against Soviet-style communism and against European colonialism. For decades, I have striven against two seemingly opposed forms of barbarism, the barbarism of hatred, domination and contempt that emerges from the depths of historical time, and the icy cold barbarism of our civilization, of the hegemony of unfettered profit and calculation. I was able to resist the war hysteria that criminalized all Germans, then the hysteria of Stalinism that criminalized all criticism of communism, and I shall resist the new forms of hysteria.

In France today, I see the need to fight on two fronts: on the one hand, against xenophobia, racism, Islamophobia, antisemitism, those barbarisms of modern civilization, and on the other, against murderous fanaticisms that contain within themselves all the ancient barbarism. The fight on this second front involves, obviously, the suppression of murderous violence. It also involves the prevention of such violence. This prevention would require policymaking on the banlieus, the reduction of social and economic inequality and the revival of a humanist education.

What is to be done in schools specifically, in the realm of pedagogy?

It is in this respect that I have proposed, since the emergence of Islamic terrorism, that the prerequisites of critical thinking be integrated into school curriculums.

The first prerequisite is the capacity to question the world. This is very present among children but can weaken with age. It must be encouraged.

Once the desire to question the world is stimulated, it is necessary to foster the act of problematizing. Problematizing serves to question things that may seem absolute to us, whether they are natural ‘verities’ (such as the sun’s apparent movement around the earth), or cultural and social ‘necessities’ (such as a dictatorial power’s legitimacy, the belief in racial superiority). Let us remember that problematizing the world was the key virtue of the European Renaissance from which science emerged. It was from problematizing God that philosophy took wing. Problematizing all authoritative judgment resulted in the spirit of democracy and the citizen. It is in such problematizing that secularism has its roots.

Critical thinking thus requires a robust capacity to question the world and to problematize. Critical thinking also requires the capacity for self-scrutiny that schooling must stimulate, so that every student is capable of the reflexivity that affords self-critique. Critical thinking without self-critique risks falling into an uncontrolled critique of all that is outside oneself. What would critical thinking be without self-critique?

Critical thinking necessarily requires a rational mind, that is, a mind capable of applying induction, deduction and logic in any examination of facts or data. The rational mind equally requires a consciousness of the limits of logic in the face of realities that can only be recognized by accepting their contradictions or by associating antagonistic terms.

Critical thinking thus nurtured by all these prerequisites can and should be exercised freely. However, it must also involve the capacity to critique critique itself, when the latter becomes intemperate or focuses only on the bad aspects of phenomena, realities or ideas. Finally, the teaching of critical thinking will involve the critique of teaching itself. Thus, critical thinking involves an entire intellectual infrastructure that is generally ignored.

These are major reforms, beginning with the reform of ways of thinking. Do you think they are achievable?

As I mentioned, the conjuncture is regressive. Extant antagonisms only reinforce each other. I never cease to recall that the previous two decades involved grave regressions on political, economic, social, ethical and intellectual fronts: a generalized crisis of democracy; fresh persecutions of religious minorities (in China, in India); the hegemony of profit; economic ravages that incited popular revolts (all of these suppressed, as in Algeria and Belarus); the domination of a certain type of thinking, based on calculation and hyperspecialization, that renders us incapable of conceptualizing and understanding the complexity of human problems, whether on an individual, national or planetary scale.

We do not know if the new American presidency will help decrease the antagonism between the U.S. and China and the tensions between the ayatollahs' Iran and the U.S.-Israeli-Saudi Arabian coalition. Turkey has become a force of Islamic interventionism in the Mediterranean. China’s imperialist tendencies are destroying Hong Kong’s autonomy and producing conflict with India. An ethno-religious war has broken out between Azerbaijan and Armenia. The crisis of the plural Lebanese state continues. Arms races break out everywhere. Europe is unable to overcome its disunion.

Hopes wane of a great ecological awakening, of a grand reform of the globalization that has created a generalized interdependence without solidarity. Rather than a fallback to a past status quo, we witness a process of regression. The impetus is feeble towards a renaissance of a political thought that would show us a new democratic, economic and ecological way.

Instead, Manichaean thinking and fanaticisms are on the rise. Nationalism and racism worsen. And simultaneously, global warming intensifies the crisis of the biosphere which will compound humanity’s crisis. Indeed, we are in the midst of a massive planetary crisis of biological, economic, civilizational and anthropological dimensions. It impacts all nations and all of humanity.

Now, I have often said that a crisis provokes, on the one hand, creativity and the imagining of novel solutions and, on the other, fear and anguish that favor regression and dictatorships. If the great regression continues, we are headed towards post-democratic systems equipped with multiple means of controlling people, techniques that are already in place within the Chinese model.

The probable course of events is extremely worrying. One cannot even exclude the hypothesis of an upheaval that becomes generalized, such as an accident of the type that occurred in Sarajevo, involving new modes of warfare conducted from computers, by hacking the networks of enemy states, via robot battles and, worse, nuclear missiles. However, the improbable may yet alter the course of history.

Can we make the improbable happen in France?

I believe in the need to organize and to unite oases of resistance, of life and thought that would continue to hold out the possibility of a change of course. We must not ourselves lapse into the thinking we denounce. I lived through the somnambulism of the disastrous 1930s. Today, the perils are very different but are, nonetheless, huge. We are subject to a new somnambulism. Heraclitus once formulated it thus: awake, they sleep.
