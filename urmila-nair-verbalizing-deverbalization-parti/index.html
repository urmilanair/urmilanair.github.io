Description

Deverbalization is a mysterious—one might even say liminal—stage that translation theorists have argued occurs during translation. It was the interpreter and translator Danica Seleskovitch who presented the most compelling discussion of this idea, arguing that deverbalization (Fr. déverbalization) is an intermediate stage of any act of translation. During deverbalization, she asserted, the meaning of the source text that the translator has comprehended is stripped of its linguistic form; it is deverbalized before it may be re-expressed in the target language. Reflexive translators will no doubt attest to some such experience in their translation practice. I wrote this paper with a view to thinking through this mysterious notion—towards verbalizing deverbalization as it were.


Abstract

The interpretive theory of translation (théorie interprétative du sens) was founded at ESIT in Paris in the 1960s. Its point of departure lay in a critique of structural linguistics that dominated back then, focusing on autonomous, systemic conceptions of language, to the exclusion of language as used. Starting instead from experience, the interpretive theorists proposed a three-stage model of translation and interpretation, with a mysterious intermediate stage that they named deverbalization, in which meaning (sens) was divested of linguistic form. The paths taken and eschewed have shaped the theory’s development, and have resulted in a present-day impasse: the theory is unable to conceptualize adequately its most compelling concept, that of deverbalization. Tracing a critical history of the theory, this article proposes a new path, towards theorizing deverbalization. Drawing on Jakobsonian and semiotic critiques of autonomous, systemic language models, the article aims to aid translators in their evaluation of translational choices, by furnishing them with adequate conceptual tools rooted in interpretive theoretical and semiotic traditions.

Key words

deverbalization; interpretive theory of translation; three-stage model of translation; Jakobsonian bipolar structure of language; semiotic nature of the linguistic sign


The article, part I (without bibliography)


Introduction

“De l’expérience aux concepts” (“From experience to concepts”) is the title of one of Danica Seleskovitch’s seminal articles on the interpretive theory of translation1. An interpreter and translator, Seleskovitch put forward the theory in the face of prevailing linguistic theories focused on systemic conceptions of language and disregarding entirely language-in-use, the necessary object of study of any translation theorist. Thus it was that Seleskovitch, followed by her colleagues and students at the École Supérieure d’Interprètes et de Traducteurs (ESIT) in Paris, rejected those dominant linguistic approaches in favor of experience as a point of departure towards concepts adequate to theorizing the practice of translation and interpretation, with the aim of aiding practitioners of these two métiers2.

One of Seleskovitch’s key propositions was that the act of translation involves a mysterious intermediate step that she termed deverbalization (déverbalisation). This step occurred between the initial comprehension of the meaning of the source text and the re-expression of that meaning in the target language. Seleskovitch explains the step thus:

“les formes linguistiques de l’original s’évanouissent pour ne laisser subsister que la conscience du sens. … l’on n’utilise pas les langues de la même manière pour désigner des notions et pour formuler des idées” (“the linguistic forms of the original disappear, leaving behind only an awareness of the meaning…. languages are not used in the same manner to designate notions and to formulate ideas”; Seleskovitch 2014/1976: 106, 108)3.

Indeed, one encounters the notion of deverbalization to this day in the hallways and classrooms of ESIT, with students and lecturers regularly invoking it in discussions of their practice. Yet, even as they invoke it, they acknowledge quite uniformly that the concept remains rather mysterious.

This article takes up this mysterious concept and attempts to shed historical and theoretical light on it. We begin with a sketch of the theory itself—its origins in the 1960s, its subsequent elaborations through interdisciplinary collaboration and the attempts at extending it, towards addressing new questions. As we shall see, on the one hand, the interpretive theorists' methodological stance, rooted in experience as it is, yielded important insights into the study of translation, and language more broadly. On the other hand, the various historical choices made by these theorists, at least partly related to their methodological stance, have created some fundamental problems for the theory. The interpretive theory is, for instance, ultimately unable to theorize its compelling concept of deverbalization. It is also unable to theorize other practical questions such as that of translating style.

Having sketched the above critical history of the interpretive theory of translation, the article will conclude with a suggestion regarding how the theory may be developed along a fresh pathway that is in keeping with its foundational methodology of beginning with experience. The aim of the suggestion is to verbalize deverbalization as it were, to conceptualize it with rigor. To this end, we shall draw on the theorization of linguist Roman Jakobson, for Jakobson’s starting point, which lies in his critique of Saussure’s model of langue, bears a family resemblance to the interpretive theory’s own point of departure.


An outline of the interpretive theory of translation

Acts of translation and interpretation must, of course, be located squarely in the domain of experience, of language-in-use as I shall term it here. This is the domain of what the interpretive theorists term parole or discourse, using both terms interchangeably (cf. for example Lederer 2014: 11)4. The problem that Seleskovitch confronted in the 1960s was that the dominant linguistic theories of the time focused almost exclusively on systemic notions of language, utterly disregarding language-in-use (Seleskovitch & Lederer 1989: 243 ff.). Marianne Lederer, her chief collaborator, references in an impressionistic fashion the prevailing “toute puissance de la linguistique structurale” of the 1960s (the “omnipotence of structural linguistics”; Lederer 2005: 3). Seleskovitch herself mentions in passing the structuralist theories of Saussure, Bloomfield and Chomsky (Seleskovitch 2014/1976: 128-129).

Noteworthy is the fact that the interpretive theory’s point of departure from those dominant structuralist theories is never specified beyond these vague, impressionistic evocations: the interpretive theorists never pick a specific theorist whose model they part from in specifically delineated ways. Thus, although they speak of langue, they neither adduce nor propose any theorization of it5.

Working thus, Seleskovitch proposed a model of the acts of translation and interpretation in three stages: comprehension, deverbalization and re-expression (compréhension, déverbalisation, réexpression; cf. for instance Seleskovitch 2014/1979: 118; Seleskovitch & Lederer 1989: 38). The first stage is that of the comprehension by the translator of the meaning (sens) of the source text within its context. The second stage is that of deverbalization, during which the translator frees herself from the linguistic forms of that meaning comprehended: these forms disappear, leaving behind solely the “deverbalized meaning” (le sens déverbalisé). The third stage is that of re-expression, wherein the translator re-expresses the deverbalized meaning in the target language.

The three-stage model is pervaded by a distinct individualism: the experience of comprehension and re-expression, and a fortiori that of deverbalization, pertain to the analytical level of the individual, the individual who encounters the text—whether oral of written—within its context6.
