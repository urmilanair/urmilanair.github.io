Description

Deverbalization is a mysterious—one might even say liminal—stage that translation theorists have argued occurs during translation. It was the interpreter and translator Danica Seleskovitch who presented the most compelling discussion of this idea, arguing that deverbalization (Fr. déverbalization) is an intermediate stage of any act of translation. During deverbalization, she asserted, the meaning of the source text that the translator has comprehended is stripped of its linguistic form; it is deverbalized before it may be re-expressed in the target language. Reflexive translators will no doubt attest to some such experience in their translation practice. I wrote this paper with a view to thinking through this mysterious notion—towards verbalizing deverbalization as it were.


Abstract

The interpretive theory of translation (théorie interprétative du sens) was founded at ESIT in Paris in the 1960s. Its point of departure lay in a critique of structural linguistics that dominated back then, focusing on autonomous, systemic conceptions of language, to the exclusion of language as used. Starting instead from experience, the interpretive theorists proposed a three-stage model of translation and interpretation, with a mysterious intermediate stage that they named deverbalization, in which meaning (sens) was divested of linguistic form. The paths taken and eschewed have shaped the theory’s development, and have resulted in a present-day impasse: the theory is unable to conceptualize adequately its most compelling concept, that of deverbalization. Tracing a critical history of the theory, this article proposes a new path, towards theorizing deverbalization. Drawing on Jakobsonian and semiotic critiques of autonomous, systemic language models, the article aims to aid translators in their evaluation of translational choices, by furnishing them with adequate conceptual tools rooted in interpretive theoretical and semiotic traditions.

Key words

deverbalization; interpretive theory of translation; three-stage model of translation; Jakobsonian bipolar structure of language; semiotic nature of the linguistic sign

Three elements are key to the three-stage model of translation proposed by the interpretive theory: meaning, linguistic signification, and linguistic form (sens, signification linguistique, forme linguistique)1. Lederer discusses these as follows:

“Ce que nous appelons sens n’est pas ce que désignent par ce mot les études sémantiques ou lexicographiques qui définissent les contours conceptuels des mots ou des structures grammaticales — ce sens-là, que nous appellerons « signification linguistique », correspond au sens des mots en dehors de l’usage qui en est fait dans la parole. Le sens de la parole, celui que transmet le message, ne se retrouve pas de manière discrète dans chaque mot, dans chaque phrase. Le sens s’appuie sur les significations linguistiques, mais il ne s’y limite pas, et c’est l’ensemble du texte qui se déroule à la lecture qui permettra de comprendre le vouloir-dire de l’auteur. " (“What we call meaning (sens) is not what semantics and lexicography designate by this term. They define meaning in terms of the conceptual contours of words or grammatical structures, which is what we will term “linguistic signification” (signification linguistique), which corresponds to the meaning of words outside their usage in parole (speech). The meaning of parole, that which communicates a message, is not to be found piecemeal in each word, in each sentence. This meaning depends on linguistic signification but is not limited to the latter, and it is the text as a whole, unfolding in the reading, that enables the comprehension of that which the author wishes to say (le vouloir-dire de l’auteur)"; Lederer 2014: 12; cf. Seleskovitch 2014/1981: 364).

Thus, for the interpretive theorists, meaning pertains to language-in-use while linguistic signification pertains to language-as-system or langue. Furthermore, linguistic signification may be polysemic and open-ended, but meaning, context-bound as it is, is always unambiguous (Seleskovitch 2014/1982: 411; Seleskovitch & Lederer 1989: 74-75). As for linguistic form, it is a langue-level element and is the material basis of language-in-use. Seleskovitch describes it thus: “la forme, support matériel du discours” (“form, the material basis of discourse”; Seleskovitch 2014/1982: 412).

The interpretive theorists adduce numerous contextual factors that are implicated in the constitution of this meaning and its comprehension: linguistic competence and knowledge are merely the starting point. Equally crucial are the context (situation de discours), cognitive complements, the cognitive baggage of the translator or interpreter as well as that of the recipient of the text, the genius of a language (le génie de la langue), that which is logically habitual to a language (les habitudes logiques d’une langue)2.

Given the three aforementioned elements that act within a text together with the various contextual factors, how does one comprehend the meaning of a particular text within its context? The three-stage model, as we have seen, locates meaning in the domain of language-in-use (parole or discourse), and on the level of individual experience. The material basis of this meaning is furnished by linguistic form, which subsists on the analytical level of langue or language-as-system. Linguistic signification, which also subsists in langue, is merely the “denominational aspect” (aspect dénominateur) of any meaning (Seleskovitch 2014/1979: 351).

In addition to the systemic and individual levels, Seleskovitch and Lederer conceptualize a third analytical level that is necessary to the constitution of meaning:

“La langue à proprement parler ne commence qu’avec l’institutionnalisation des sens créés par le discours; elle prend alors sa revanche et impose sa loi en devenant objet d’acquisition et en fixant leurs ultimes limites aux emplois des mots.” (“Language in its strict sense does not begin except with the institutionalization of meanings created by discourse. It then takes its revenge and imposes its law, becoming the object of acquisition and fixing limits to the uses of words."; Seleskovitch 2014/1976: 132).

We see indicated here the third analytical level on which language operates, by means of which language “in its strict sense,” begins to have meaning. This is the level of institutionalized, shared norms and conventions, in short, the level of the group3. Lederer discusses this third level further:

“Si l’on définit la connaissance linguistique comme une compréhension ponctuelle des significations verbales, et le savoir comme l’ensemble des paraphrases et exégèses que l’on pourrait faire d’une notion donnée (qui pourrait dans certains cas remplir plusieurs volumes), on voit qu’il n’y a pas de solution de continuité entre connaissance linguistique et savoir et qu’en étiquetant les choses, la langue n’apporte que le début du savoir” (“If one defines linguistic knowledge (connaissance linguistique) as the punctual comprehension of verbal signification, and savoir as the entirety of the paraphrases and exegeses that one could have regarding a given notion (which could in certain cases fill several volumes), one sees then that there is no continuity possible between linguistic knowledge and learning, and that langue, by labeling things, yields merely the beginning of savoir"; Lederer 2014: 16)

The savoir of which Lederer speaks here pertains to the third analytical level of the group: it is a voluminous knowledge, of the ensemble of paraphrases and exegeses that can fill tomes in relation to any given notion. An individual would, presumably, only possess a portion of a group’s savoir at any given time. Note that Lederer opposes this group knowledge of savoir to “linguistic knowledge” (connaissance linguistique), which is a punctual knowledge of “verbal signification,” presumably referring to the knowledge that an individual may experientially possess of langue. Crucially, linguistic knowledge is thus discontinuous with savoir: the two subsist on distinct analytical levels (viz. those of the individual and the group).

It is through the operation of language “in its strict sense” on these three analytical levels that linguistic signs (on the level of langue) are able to mean (on the level of individual experience). For the linguistic sign possesses both a verbal signification (known punctually to individuals on the level of individual experience of system-level signs) and a shared meaning (institutionalized on the group-level). For example, the exclamation “Bientôt samedi !” (“Saturday soon!") can mean “Les vacances !” (“The holidays!") to students on a Thursday at the end of the semester. A blue butterfly may be meaningfully described as “a case of beer” by a legionnaire of the French Foreign Legion who could sell that rare specimen for the price of the latter in the markets of French Guiana (Seleskovitch 2014/1979: 346-348).
