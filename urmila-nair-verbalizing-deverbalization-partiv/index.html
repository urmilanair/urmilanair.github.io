Description

Deverbalization is a mysterious—one might even say liminal—stage that translation theorists have argued occurs during translation. It was the interpreter and translator Danica Seleskovitch who presented the most compelling discussion of this idea, arguing that deverbalization (Fr. déverbalization) is an intermediate stage of any act of translation. During deverbalization, she asserted, the meaning of the source text that the translator has comprehended is stripped of its linguistic form; it is deverbalized before it may be re-expressed in the target language. Reflexive translators will no doubt attest to some such experience in their translation practice. I wrote this paper with a view to thinking through this mysterious notion—towards verbalizing deverbalization as it were.


Abstract

The interpretive theory of translation (théorie interprétative du sens) was founded at ESIT in Paris in the 1960s. Its point of departure lay in a critique of structural linguistics that dominated back then, focusing on autonomous, systemic conceptions of language, to the exclusion of language as used. Starting instead from experience, the interpretive theorists proposed a three-stage model of translation and interpretation, with a mysterious intermediate stage that they named deverbalization, in which meaning (sens) was divested of linguistic form. The paths taken and eschewed have shaped the theory’s development, and have resulted in a present-day impasse: the theory is unable to conceptualize adequately its most compelling concept, that of deverbalization. Tracing a critical history of the theory, this article proposes a new path, towards theorizing deverbalization. Drawing on Jakobsonian and semiotic critiques of autonomous, systemic language models, the article aims to aid translators in their evaluation of translational choices, by furnishing them with adequate conceptual tools rooted in interpretive theoretical and semiotic traditions.

Key words

deverbalization; interpretive theory of translation; three-stage model of translation; Jakobsonian bipolar structure of language; semiotic nature of the linguistic sign

A way out of the impasse

How might the interpretive theory of translation get out of this impasse? Let us consider the work of linguist Roman Jakobson whose theorization is much cited by the interpretive theorists, though unfortunately in a piecemeal fashion and, consequently, to no avail (cf. for example Israël 1994: 108, 1990: 38 ff.). We begin with Seleskovitch’s critique of Jakobson in her seminal essay cited at the outset of this article. Seleskovitch chooses to class Jakobson with the rest of the structuralist linguists, dismissing him as one of those theorists who “focus exclusively on the level of langue” ("qui se situent au seul plan de la langue"), who never consider a “real situation” ("une situation réelle") within which a sentence might derive its meaning (Seleskovitch 2014/1976: 116-118). Let us consider what Jakobson writes before discussing Seleskovitch’s reading of it:

“If some grammatical category is absent in a given language, its meaning may be translated into this language by lexical means…. It is more difficult to remain faithful to the original when we translate into a language provided with a certain grammatical category from a language devoid of such a category…. As Boas neatly observed, the grammatical pattern of a language (as opposed to its lexical stock) determines those aspects of each experience that must be expressed in the given language … In order to translate accurately the English sentence “I hired a worker,” a Russian needs supplementary information … because he must make his choice between a verb of completive or noncompletive aspect … and between a masculine and feminine noun … Because the information required by the English and Russian grammatical pattern is unlike, we face quite different sets of two-choice situations” (Jakobson 2012: 129).

Seleskovitch cites the above passage and argues that the “real situation” of an utterance like “I hired a worker” (she cites the sentence in English) would indicate to the Russian translator the gender of the worker to be used in the translation despite the absence of that information in the English. While this is not incorrect, her reading misses Jakobson’s point.

In fact, what Jakobson gestures towards here is precisely a means of theorizing a fundamental aspect of the stage of deverbalization. He does not conceptualize this stage as such, and he does not discuss “interlingual translation” as being anything other than a two-stage process—which would have been grounds for an interesting critique of his essay by Seleskovitch, though unfortunately she does not pursue that thread.

The passage cited above from Jakobson’s essay on translation in fact offers an explanation of one of the reasons why any reflexive translator, while translating, would have noticed an intermediate stage that could be named “deverbalization”: different languages express and parse the world differently, for instance via different “grammatical patterns”. Jakobson gestures here towards his own theory of language, the starting point of which is a critique of the Saussurean dichotomy of langue versus parole, a critique that bears a family resemblance to Seleskovitch’s own critique of dominant structuralist linguistic theories of the 1960s1.

In what follows, we shall visit Jakobson’s theorization of language. Our aim is to think through the interpretive theory’s concept of deverbalization.

Jakobson on language

One of Jakobson’s fundamental critiques of Saussure begins with the observation of shifters. Shifters “are distinguished from all other constituents of the linguistic code solely by their compulsory reference to the given message” (Jakobson 1971: 132; Caton 1987: 225ff.). For instance, the pronoun “I” indexes the person uttering it but is simultaneously a linguistic sign. In the face of shifters, the Saussurean langue-parole dichotomy begins to crumble (cf. also Benveniste 1966). In fact, Jakobson points out, this property—of simultaneously belonging to a language code and being indelibly dependent on the context of the latter’s use—is quite prevalent. It is to be found on all levels of language, from the phonic to the semantic to the grammatical. Language, for Jakobson, is therefore best studied not in terms of Saussurean autonomous langue, but rather as context-dependent signs involved in chains of semiosis, following the philosopher Charles Sanders Peirce.

Rather than the dichotomy of langue and parole, Jakobson configures the linguistic sign within code and message. He speaks of language as having a “bipolar structure,” organized along virtual and actual or realized axes (1987/1956: 111)2.

At the heart of Jakobson’s conception of language is his model of the speech event in which a message is communicated by an addresser to an addressee, the two being somehow in contact, within a specific context, using a shared code3. Such a speech event involves six factors: addresser, addressee, message, context, contact, code. Each factor “determines a different function of language” (Jakobson 1987/1960: 66-71). Every verbal message involves all six of these functions, though one or more may predominate. While we shall not detail the six functions, one of them may be cited for illustrative purposes: consider a speech event aimed at communicating a certain piece of information. In Jakobsonian terms, the set (Einstellung)4 of this verbal communication is towards the context or referent. The dominant function involved is therefore the referential or denotative or cognitive function, which shapes the message in light of this focus. Of course, other functions could play secondary roles therein as well. For instance, the emotive or expressive function of language, with its set towards the addresser, could also play a role, nuancing the information with the addresser’s feelings about the subject.

Consider the factor that Jakobson terms code within the speech event. Code here “represents a system of interconnected subcodes” which are experienced by speakers as “a unity of language” (Jakobson 1987/1960: 65) 5.

The linguistic signs that make up language codes subsist along two axes. First, the linguistic sign subsists virtually in a code. At the moment of a speech event, the sign must be selected from this virtual set. Other terms that describe this axis of selection are: equivalence, similarity, substitution, alternation, metaphor. For the linguistic sign is selected based on its equivalence or similarity to the other signs of that virtual set; it can substitute for or alternate with them, and stands in a relation of metaphor to them. The second axis along which the linguistic sign subsists is the axis of combination: the linguistic sign selected from that virtual set is actualized in the message. Other terms that describe this axis are: contexture, contiguity, alignment, metonymy. For the linguistic sign selected is combined contiguously with the other signs of the message; it is aligned with them within the context of the message; and it stands in a relation of metonymy with respect to the other signs of the message. These two modes of arrangement obtain at all levels of language—phonetic, semantic and grammatical. This is language’s bipolar structure.

For example, an invocation of “un papillon bleu” (“a blue butterfly”) could be a sign within a message between two legionnaires, functioning referentially or cognitively to mean “une caisse de bière” (“a case of beer”) within their shared subcode (Seleskovitch 2014/1979: 348). This meaning would not obtain within the wider French language code but could be easily explained to French non-legionnaires using other equivalent linguistic signs. Additionally, “un papillon bleu” used thus would semiotically index the addresser and addressee’s sharedness of that subcode and membership within the Foreign Legion in French Guiana.

By virtue of these two modes of arrangement, the linguistic sign is involved in two sets of semiotic relations within a message (Jakobson 1987/1956: 98-100, 104-106). One relation is between the linguistic sign and the code to which it belongs. This is an internal relation. As was mentioned, it is virtual and based on equivalence, the sign being selected from a virtual set containing signs that are similar, whether phonically (pig, fig) or semantically (kid, child, infant; or money, a case of beer, blue butterfly) or grammatically (parallel grammatical categories used in poetry). The second relation is an external one, between the linguistic sign and the context of the message. This is the relationship of actual contiguity between the signs selected, which combine to form the message within its context of the speech event.

Crucial to Jakobson’s conception is the semiotic nature of the linguistic sign, in the Peircean sense. The linguistic sign is involved in chains of semiosis at all linguistic levels, from the phonemic to the textual6. A simple example is the sentence “the President and the Secretary of State attended the meeting,” in which the two are always mentioned in this order. The reason is that the relative positions of the two noun phrases function as a sign, indexing an extra-linguistic social hierarchy (Jakobson 1987/1956: 98-100, 104-106; 1987/1966: 418-421).

This schema of Jakobson’s conceptualization of language suffices for our purposes here. It serves to indicate that Jakobson moved away from a langue-parole distinction and developed a rigorous, experience- and observation-based theorization of language in all its “bipolar” and semiotic complexity.
