Description

Deverbalization is a mysterious—one might even say liminal—stage that translation theorists have argued occurs during translation. It was the interpreter and translator Danica Seleskovitch who presented the most compelling discussion of this idea, arguing that deverbalization (Fr. déverbalization) is an intermediate stage of any act of translation. During deverbalization, she asserted, the meaning of the source text that the translator has comprehended is stripped of its linguistic form; it is deverbalized before it may be re-expressed in the target language. Reflexive translators will no doubt attest to some such experience in their translation practice. I wrote this paper with a view to thinking through this mysterious notion—towards verbalizing deverbalization as it were.


Abstract

The interpretive theory of translation (théorie interprétative du sens) was founded at ESIT in Paris in the 1960s. Its point of departure lay in a critique of structural linguistics that dominated back then, focusing on autonomous, systemic conceptions of language, to the exclusion of language as used. Starting instead from experience, the interpretive theorists proposed a three-stage model of translation and interpretation, with a mysterious intermediate stage that they named deverbalization, in which meaning (sens) was divested of linguistic form. The paths taken and eschewed have shaped the theory’s development, and have resulted in a present-day impasse: the theory is unable to conceptualize adequately its most compelling concept, that of deverbalization. Tracing a critical history of the theory, this article proposes a new path, towards theorizing deverbalization. Drawing on Jakobsonian and semiotic critiques of autonomous, systemic language models, the article aims to aid translators in their evaluation of translational choices, by furnishing them with adequate conceptual tools rooted in interpretive theoretical and semiotic traditions.

Key words

deverbalization; interpretive theory of translation; three-stage model of translation; Jakobsonian bipolar structure of language; semiotic nature of the linguistic sign

Verbalizing Deverbalization

How may we mobilize Jakobson’s conceptions towards verbalizing, as it were, deverbalization? To recapitulate, Seleskovitch conceptualized deverbalization as a moment of “pensée non verbale” (“non-verbal thought”; Seleskovitch 2014/1976: 89). In this stage, the meaning of the source text, comprehended during the first, comprehension stage of translation, subsists as “deverbalized meaning” (sens déverbalisé).

Now, crucially, Jakobson holds that it is not merely languages that possess a bipolar structure but all semiotic systems. He writes of the virtual-actual dichotomy that it “appears to be of primal significance and consequence for all verbal behavior and for human behavior in general” (Jakobson 1987/1956: 112)1. Drawing on this conception, we posit the following: during deverbalization, the translator reflects on a bipolar structure of equivalences across two sets of codes, with a view to selecting from amongst those equivalences. The equivalences across the two codes pertain to all levels of language, including extra-linguistic sociocultural realities that are encoded in language2. The equivalences are established in and by the conventions of second language acquisition as well as institutional forms (lexicons, grammar texts), and are manifest in the linguistic competence and knowledge, and the cognitive baggage of the translator.

Let us consider the example of a French to English translation of the legionnaires' “un papillon bleu” (“a blue butterfly”), cited earlier, towards clarifying this position3.

Consider first the stages of comprehension and re-expression. The translator begins by comprehending that “un papillon bleu” means a sum of money, specifically, the price of a case of beer. Terms belonging to other subcodes and to the French language code more broadly, subsisting virtually alongside “papillon bleu” on the axis of selection, include: pognon, fric, oseille, argent, monnaie, etc.

The re-expression of “un papillon bleu” in English would depend on the context of the translation. The virtual set of equivalent terms would include: money, cash, funds, buck, pony, dough, bacon. In the subtitles of a movie about legionnaires, perhaps involving a visual of the blue butterfly, the translator may forego the imagery and sociocultural specificities of the sign and its subcode, translating it simply as “cash,” for rapid comprehension by the viewer. In case of an academic translation, the translator could offer a literal translation of the equivalence with an explanatory gloss—as has been done in this article for example. In a novel, absent any reference to Lepidoptera, the translator might choose to preserve the logic of evaluating animal species in terms of alcohol, and use “bucks,” since folk tradition has it that in the 18^th^ century in the United States, five bucks—referring to their skins—were worth a cask of whiskey (Kenton 2020). Alternatively, if the translation was for a British audience, and the aim was to preserve a sense of the amount of money involved, she might select “pony,” British slang for £25 (Haigh 2018). The specific term selected would thus depend on the verbal communication within which the translation was to occur.

Consider now an example of the equivalences across linguistic and cultural codes and subcodes on which the translator reflects during deverbalization. As mentioned, these equivalences pertain to all the levels of the codes involved, from the phonetic to the semantic, the grammatical and the textual. We will consider the phonetic level here for illustrative purposes. Imagine that the French phrase to be translated is a legionnaire’s words “Un papillon bleu pour une caisse de bière!” and that the original French was included in a text that had some poetic value. The English translation would accordingly have to appeal poetically to the English reader. The translator during deverbalization would therefore have to consider various possible sound shapes. At the level of the individual words she has the following equivalence sets across the two codes:

bleu (adjective) == blue, azure, turquoise, cerulean, etc. (adjectives)

papillon (noun, masculine) == butterfly, Lepidoptera, winged-creature, insect, etc. (nouns)

She would consider the various permutations of these two and their sound shapes:

papillon bleu == blue butterfly, azure insect, cerulean winged-creature, cerulean creature

She would play with the various possibilities, arriving at the following phrase-level set of similar signs:

“Un papillon bleu pour une caisse de bière !”

==

“Blue butterflies buy beer!”

“The cerulean creature—my case of beer!”

Now “Blue butterflies buy beer!” with its alliteration may sound amusing. By contrast, “The cerulean creature–my case of beer!” produces a phonetic and cultural-conceptual dissonance between the ethereal-sounding “cerulean” and “beer,” the latter associated with down-to-earth folk in American and British culture. This dissonance could make the utterer—the legionnaire in translation—cut a ridiculous figure. The possibilities of such effects would be known to the translator through her cognitive baggage. During deverbalization, she would evaluate these various effects, reflecting on the various equivalences across the two codes. She would assess each such permutation across the two codes, for comparability of reference and of effect on the reader. At the end of deverbalization, she would select what she considers to be the best equivalence across the two codes, depending on the context, thereby arriving at a specific combination of linguistic signs to be actualized in re-expression.

The process discussed above with regard to the phonetic plane applies equally in case of equivalences at the levels of grammar and cultural concepts that are encoded in language. For instance, consider the example of “I hired a worker” and the Russian translator’s problem of specifying the noun’s grammatical gender. From a Jakobsonian perspective, during deverbalization, the translator would draw on her cognitive baggage to reflect on equivalences at the level cultural concepts: along the axis of selection would be certain types of workers culturally construed as being feminine in anglophone and Russian cultures (nurses, nannies) and others construed as masculine (plumbers, mechanics). The translator would reflect on these cultural equivalences and select the grammatical gender accordingly, for use in the axis of combination actualized in the translation.

These then are examples of the bipolar structure of equivalences across language and cultural codes on which the translator reflects during the stage of deverbalization. A discussion of these structures has given us a way to verbalize deverbalization, to conceptualize it, with a view to exploring it further, and to answering questions that the interpretive theory could not address for lack of a theorization of langue. Thus it is that, via the stage of deverbalization, the translator wends her way out of the axes of selection and combination of the source text’s code and subcodes, and gradually immerses herself in those of the target language4. She does this by reflecting on the bipolar structure of equivalences across the two sets of codes and subcodes.


Conclusion

This article has thus offered a critical history of the interpretive theory of translation, a theory founded by interpreter and translator, Danica Seleskovitch, and extended by her collaborators and students at ESIT in Paris and beyond. We traced the origins of the theory in the 1960s when, confronted by the predominant disdain for the study of language in use, Seleskovitch chose to begin with experience. She posited the mysterious stage of deverbalization as occurring betwixt and between the comprehension of a source text and its re-expression in a target language. Unfortunately, the historical foundations of the theory, and the specific paths taken and eschewed in its subsequent elaboration, entailed that deverbalization could not be adequately conceptualized. The interpretive theory suffers from the inadequate theorization of its concept of langue. For langue, in the form given it by structuralist linguistics, does not present itself as such to individual experience. The interpretive theorists, insisting as they did on the experiential basis of their theory, were therefore unable to account adequately for langue even though it lay at the heart of their three-stage model of translation.

Having sketched out this critical history of the interpretive theory, we then visited the theorization of Jakobson, rooted in a critique of Saussurean structuralism, a critique that bears a family resemblance to the starting point of the interpretive theorists. We drew on Jakobson’s conception of the “bipolar structure” of language and, indeed, all semiosis, to verbalize, as it were, deverbalization. Deverbalization, we argued, may be understood as a moment in which the translator, betwixt and between two codes, reflects on equivalences across those codes on all semiotic levels, from phonetic to semantic and grammatical to cultural: the translator reflects on potential equivalences and then selects from among them, towards actualizing a translation.

There remains, of course, much work to be done towards fleshing out deverbalization: each of the linguistic and semiotic levels mentioned above merits a thorough exploration. Next, a rigorous analysis of deverbalization in Peircean terms would no doubt yield fresh insights into the nature of translation. Furthermore, there is the fact that the act of translation in practice involves numerous iterations of the three-stage model, and this applies across the board to all types of translation (cf. for instance Williamson 2021 on an ideal of financial translation practice). A study of the three-stage model in such an iterative process represents another way forward into a well-founded, conceptually backed verbalization of deverbalization.
